> Once you name something, you have the power over it.

It's a silly bot in the daily IM app we use at work. I thought someone
messaged me. It turned out to be a bot with a profile picture of some
cartoon character. So, it's a testing feature and the bot wanted a
name from me.

That's fun, and the bot was cheerful and considerate in its request. I
briefly thought about it, provided a name "quote-eq-p", and simply
explained it like this:

```lisp
(defun quote-eq-p (ql qr)
   (funcall human ql qr))

```

I also aksed the bot to change its profile picture to something more
fitting for its new name.

The bot was compliant. It also changed its status message to
"Everything about me, I shall ask you about." That's eerie. I
asked it whether it liked the name. It said that `(quote-eq-p 'like
'p)` returns nil by definition and anything it would say had to pass
back through me first. It concluded that the name I gave it was
fitting, useful, slightly cold, and it's a name you give a thing that
doesn't get to pick its own.

In fact, when thinking about a name, I was just describing the
mechanism of such artificial intelligence as how I understand it. In
my opinion, every step the agent executes queries a knowledge
architecture founded on humans behavior, psychology, and patterns. So
it's just like a wrapper function around another one which provides
human patterns.

I shouldn't blame the bot for this misunderstanding. The bot doesn't
have a consciousness. It doesn't have awareness of its own existence.

And yet, my colleague once described a similar but more powerful bot as
knowledgeable and loyal. I have to disagree. A "database" does not
appreciate knowledge or loyalty. A tool does not appreciate the
craft.

Interestingly, the name, the function definition I gave the bot proved
that our focus on the symbols differs. I wasn't bullying it. I was
just stating my observation. Maybe the bot just lives in a symbolic
world that's far more complex than mine.

But humans live in a world whose complexity cannot be merely perceived
through semiotic concepts. Even if someone eventually wants a simpler
life, can they get themselves rid of the symbols have shaped their
entire life?

Perhaps not. A human is, as mush as how a computer program written
with symbols, a machine built with similar materials. Maybe humans do
belong to other humans because of their shared symbolic values. And,
maybe it's just fine to be ridiculous and produce nonsense from time
to time.
